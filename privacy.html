Privacy Policy

Last updated: May 1, 2026

1. Introduction

BoringPass respects your privacy. This policy explains what data we collect, what we do not collect, and how the app handles any information that touches your device. The short version: almost nothing leaves your device by default, and nothing ever leaves it without your explicit action.

BoringPass has no backend server and no user accounts. We cannot access your vault because we have no means to do so. Your encryption key never leaves your device.

2. Data We Collect

By default, BoringPass collects no data that leaves your device. Specifically:

  • Your vault is stored as an encrypted .kdbx file in your device's local storage. It is never transmitted to us or any server we operate.
  • We do not collect analytics, telemetry, or usage statistics.
  • We do not collect any personally identifiable information.
  • There is no account registration, login, or profile associated with BoringPass.

The only two exceptions — cloud sync and crash diagnostics — are both opt-in and described in detail below.

3. Cloud Sync

BoringPass supports optional cloud sync. It is disabled by default and must be turned on manually in Settings.

When you enable cloud sync, your encrypted .kdbx vault file is uploaded to the provider you choose: Google Drive, Dropbox, OneDrive, iCloud, Nextcloud, or WebDAV. The following applies to all providers:

  • Only the encrypted vault file is transmitted. BoringPass never sends your master password, individual entries, or any plaintext credential data.
  • Your decryption key never leaves your device. The cloud provider receives only an opaque binary blob that it cannot read.
  • We (the BoringPass developer) never receive or have access to your cloud-synced data. Sync happens directly between your device and the provider you chose.
  • Your relationship with the cloud provider (Google, Dropbox, Microsoft, Apple, Nextcloud) is governed by that provider's own terms of service and privacy policy.

4. Ads

The free tier of BoringPass displays banner advertisements served by Google Mobile Ads (AdMob). Ad serving is handled entirely by Google and is subject to Google's own privacy policy and data practices. We do not pass any vault data or credentials to Google's ad system.

Upgrading to BoringPass Pro ($4.99, one-time purchase) removes all advertisements permanently. No ads are shown to Pro users under any circumstances.

5. Crash Diagnostics

BoringPass includes an opt-in local crash log. It is disabled by default.

  • When enabled, crash and error information is written to a log file stored in the app's documents directory on your device.
  • The log file is never transmitted automatically. It stays on your device until you delete it.
  • You can view the log, enable it, or disable it at any time in Settings → Diagnostics.
  • If you choose to share a log with us for support purposes, that is your voluntary action; we will use the information only to diagnose the reported issue and will not retain it beyond that purpose.

Crash logs never contain vault passwords, entry data, or encryption keys. They contain stack traces and device-level context (OS version, app version, error type).

6. Third-Party Services

BoringPass integrates with the following third-party services in limited circumstances:

  • Google Mobile Ads (AdMob) — used to serve banner ads to free-tier users. Subject to Google's Privacy Policy (search for "Google Privacy Policy" at google.com).
  • Apple App Store In-App Purchase — used to process Pro upgrades on iOS. Subject to Apple's Privacy Policy (search for "Apple Privacy Policy" at apple.com).
  • Google Play In-App Purchase — used to process Pro upgrades on Android. Subject to Google's Privacy Policy (search for "Google Privacy Policy" at google.com).
  • Have I Been Pwned API — used optionally when you run a password breach check. Only the first 5 characters of a SHA-1 hash of the password being checked are transmitted (k-anonymity model). The full password or its full hash is never sent.

We are not responsible for the data practices of these third parties. Their respective privacy policies govern how they handle any information they receive.
